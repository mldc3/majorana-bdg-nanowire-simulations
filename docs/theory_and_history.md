# Theory and Historical Background

## 1. From superconductivity to the BdG description

The numerical models in this repository are based on the Bogoliubov--de Gennes (BdG) formalism. The reason BdG theory appears so naturally in Majorana physics is that a superconductor does not conserve the number of electrons in the effective quasiparticle description. Instead, the superconducting condensate allows pairs of electrons to be created and destroyed coherently. As a result, a single-particle excitation is not a pure electron: it is a coherent electron-hole superposition.

A compact way to describe this is to introduce a Nambu spinor. In the spinful nanowire model used here, the local basis is

$$
\Psi_i=
\begin{pmatrix} c_{i\uparrow}\\ c_{i\downarrow}\\ c_{i\downarrow}^{\dagger}\\ -c_{i\uparrow}^{\dagger}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The minus sign in the last component is a basis convention that makes the singlet pairing term especially compact. In this basis, the local BdG Hamiltonian contains Pauli matrices $\sigma_a$ acting on spin and $\tau_a$ acting on particle-hole space. A generic one-dimensional Rashba nanowire with Zeeman field and superconducting pairing is represented by

$$
H_{\mathrm{BdG}}= \left(\frac{p_x^2}{2m^*}-\mu+V(x)\right)\tau_z -\frac{\alpha_R}{\hbar}p_x\sigma_y\tau_z +E_Z\sigma_x +\operatorname{Re}\Delta(x)\tau_x -\operatorname{Im}\Delta(x)\tau_y .
$$

Each term has a specific physical role:

- $p_x^2/(2m^*)$ gives the kinetic energy of electrons in the effective one-dimensional semiconductor channel.
- $\mu$ sets the chemical potential relative to the band bottom.
- $V(x)$ represents electrostatic gates, barriers, disorder, smooth confinement, or uncovered normal regions.
- $\alpha_R$ is the Rashba spin-orbit coupling, generated by structural inversion asymmetry and electric fields.
- $E_Z$ is the Zeeman energy produced by an applied magnetic field.
- $\Delta(x)$ is the superconducting pairing amplitude, either computed self-consistently or imposed as an induced proximity gap.

The BdG Hamiltonian has an intrinsic particle-hole redundancy. If $\psi_n$ is an eigenstate at energy $E_n$, the particle-hole transformed state is an eigenstate at $-E_n$. This symmetry is not optional; it follows from writing the problem in a basis that includes both electron and hole degrees of freedom. Numerically, it is a crucial validation test: the spectrum should be symmetric around zero energy up to floating-point error.

## 2. Historical motivation: Majorana fermions and topological superconductors

The word Majorana originates from Ettore Majorana's 1937 proposal of real solutions of a relativistic fermionic equation. In condensed-matter systems, the relevant object is not a fundamental particle but an emergent quasiparticle operator. A Majorana operator $\gamma$ satisfies

$$
\gamma^{\dagger}=\gamma,
\qquad
\{\gamma_i,\gamma_j\}=2\delta_{ij}.
$$

A usual complex fermion can always be decomposed into two Majorana operators:

$$
f=\frac{\gamma_1+i\gamma_2}{2},
\qquad
f^{\dagger}=\frac{\gamma_1-i\gamma_2}{2}.
$$

The important condensed-matter question is not whether this algebraic decomposition is possible; it always is. The important question is whether the two Majorana components can be spatially separated and protected by a superconducting gap. When that happens, the occupation of the nonlocal fermion is stored in a pair of distant boundary modes. This nonlocal encoding is the physical reason Majorana zero modes are interesting for topological quantum computation.

The modern one-dimensional route to Majoranas starts from the Kitaev chain: a spinless p-wave superconducting lattice model. Later proposals showed that an effective p-wave topological superconductor can be engineered using a semiconductor nanowire with strong spin-orbit coupling, a Zeeman field, and ordinary s-wave superconductivity induced by proximity. The spin-orbit coupling and Zeeman field convert a conventional induced pairing into an effective spinless pairing channel over an appropriate range of parameters.

## 3. The Kitaev chain: the minimal model

The Kitaev chain is the simplest lattice model supporting Majorana zero modes. It is a one-dimensional chain of spinless fermions with nearest-neighbor hopping, chemical potential, and p-wave superconducting pairing:

$$
H=-\mu\sum_j c_j^{\dagger}c_j -t\sum_j\left(c_j^{\dagger}c_{j+1}+c_{j+1}^{\dagger}c_j\right) +\Delta\sum_j\left(c_j c_{j+1}+c_{j+1}^{\dagger}c_j^{\dagger}\right).
$$

The normal term $t$ delocalizes particles across the chain. The chemical potential $\mu$ controls the filling. The p-wave pairing $\Delta$ couples neighboring sites and is antisymmetric under exchange, as required for spinless fermions. In the code, the normal and pairing matrices are built explicitly in real space:

$$
h_{jj}=-\mu, \qquad h_{j,j+1}=h_{j+1,j}=-t,
$$

and

$$
\Delta_{j,j+1}=+\Delta,
\qquad
\Delta_{j+1,j}=-\Delta.
$$

The BdG matrix then has the block structure

$$
H_{\mathrm{BdG}}= \begin{pmatrix} h & \Delta\\ -\Delta^* & -h^* \end{pmatrix}.
$$

For an infinite translationally invariant chain, Fourier transformation gives the bulk dispersion

$$
E(k)=\pm\sqrt{\left(-\mu-2t\cos k\right)^2+ \left(2\Delta\sin k\right)^2}.
$$

The bulk gap closes when the expression under the square root can vanish. Since $\sin k=0$ at $k=0,\pi$, the gap closing occurs at

$$
\mu=-2t \quad \text{or} \quad \mu=+2t.
$$

The topological phase is therefore

$$
|\mu|<2|t|,
\qquad
\Delta\neq 0.
$$

This condition appears directly in the Kitaev-chain script as the ideal topological criterion. The finite-chain simulations then test the bulk-boundary correspondence: when the infinite system is topological, an open finite chain should develop exponentially localized boundary modes with energies approaching zero as the chain length increases.

## 4. Majorana decomposition and finite-size splitting

In a finite chain, the two Majorana end modes are not perfectly independent. Their wavefunctions overlap through the bulk, which produces an exponentially small splitting away from zero energy. The near-zero Dirac fermion can be decomposed into two Majorana components by combining the positive- and negative-energy particle-hole partner states. Conceptually, if $\psi_+$ and $\psi_-$ are the two partner states near zero, one forms real particle-hole symmetric combinations analogous to

$$
\gamma_L\sim\psi_+ + \psi_-,
\qquad
\gamma_R\sim -i(\psi_+ - \psi_-).
$$

The exact numerical implementation must align the arbitrary global phases of the eigenvectors before taking these combinations. Otherwise, the visualized Majorana components can be contaminated by meaningless phase conventions. The code handles this by applying the particle-hole transformation, aligning phases through the inner product, and then constructing two real Majorana-like spatial densities.

The energy splitting is expected to decay approximately as

$$
E_{\mathrm{split}}(L)\sim A e^{-L/\xi}\cos(k_F L+\phi),
$$

where $\xi$ is a localization length and $k_F$ is an effective Fermi momentum. The exponential factor reflects the decreasing overlap between left and right end modes. The oscillatory factor appears because the wavefunctions can oscillate inside the superconducting coherence envelope. In practice, the numerical signature is that $\min |E|$ becomes smaller as the chain length grows, although it may not decrease monotonically for every parameter value because of the oscillatory term.

## 5. From Kitaev to spinful BdG nanowires

The Kitaev chain is conceptually clean, but it assumes spinless fermions and p-wave pairing. Real semiconductor nanowires are spinful and are usually coupled to an ordinary s-wave superconductor. The bridge between the two descriptions is the combination of three ingredients:

1. Rashba spin-orbit coupling locks spin and motion.
2. A Zeeman field breaks the Kramers degeneracy and can leave an effectively spinless low-energy band.
3. Proximity-induced s-wave pairing becomes effectively p-wave after projection into the spin-split band.

The real-space discretized BdG Hamiltonian used in the spinful models is

$$
H_{\mathrm{lat}}=
\sum_i\Psi_i^{\dagger}h_i\Psi_i+
\sum_i\left(\Psi_{i+1}^{\dagger}T\Psi_i+\Psi_i^{\dagger}T^{\dagger}\Psi_{i+1}\right),
$$

with local block

$$
h_i=(2t-\mu+V_i)\tau_z+E_Z\sigma_x+
\operatorname{Re}\Delta_i\tau_x-
\operatorname{Im}\Delta_i\tau_y,
$$

and nearest-neighbor block

$$
T=-t\tau_z-i t_{\mathrm{so}}\sigma_y\tau_z,
\qquad
t=\frac{\hbar^2}{2m^*a^2},
\qquad
t_{\mathrm{so}}=\frac{\alpha_R}{2a}.
$$

This form is central to the project. The hopping term discretizes the kinetic energy. The spin-orbit hopping is imaginary and spin-dependent because it comes from a first derivative in space. The Zeeman term mixes spin states. The pairing term mixes particle and hole sectors.

For a homogeneous proximitized wire, the ideal bulk topological threshold is often written as

$$
E_Z>\sqrt{\mu^2+\Delta^2}.
$$

This expression should be interpreted carefully. It is the clean, homogeneous, single-band criterion. In finite, inhomogeneous, or smooth-potential wires, low-energy states can appear even when the simple bulk criterion is not globally satisfied. That is why the project compares ideal phase diagrams with finite-size spectra, LDOS, localization, charge, spin, and Majorana decomposition.

## 6. Self-consistent pairing: from a fixed gap to an order parameter

The self-consistent model is the intermediate step between the ideal Kitaev chain and the experimentally motivated proximitized wire. Here the superconducting order parameter is not imposed as a constant input. Instead, it is recalculated from the BdG eigenvectors themselves.

A mean-field superconducting order parameter represents an anomalous expectation value. For singlet pairing, the local order parameter has the schematic form

$$
\Delta_i \propto \langle c_{i\downarrow}c_{i\uparrow}\rangle.
$$

After solving the BdG eigenproblem, each eigenvector contains electron amplitudes $u_{n\sigma}(i)$ and hole amplitudes $v_{n\sigma}(i)$. The local pairing update used in the code is

$$
\Delta_i^{\mathrm{new}}=\frac{V_{\mathrm{eff}}}{2}
\sum_{|E_n|<E_c}
\left[u_{n\uparrow}(i)v^*_{n\downarrow}(i)-u_{n\downarrow}(i)v^*_{n\uparrow}(i)\right]
\tanh\left(\frac{E_n}{2k_BT}\right).
$$

The factor $V_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is an effective attractive interaction. It is not a microscopic Coulomb interaction calculated from first principles; it is a controlled phenomenological parameter that sets how strongly anomalous correlations feed back into the order parameter. The cutoff $E_c$ restricts the update to quasiparticle states close enough to the Fermi level to participate in pairing. The hyperbolic tangent encodes thermal occupation: at low temperature, positive and negative quasiparticle branches contribute with nearly saturated signs, while at higher temperature the pairing correlations are reduced.

The numerical loop is:

1. choose an initial seed $\Delta_i^{(0)}$;
2. build $H_{\mathrm{BdG}}[\Delta^{(k)}]$;
3. diagonalize the matrix;
4. compute $\Delta_i^{\mathrm{new}}$ from the eigenvectors;
5. mix the update with the previous profile,

$$
\Delta_i^{(k+1)}=(1-\eta)\Delta_i^{(k)}+\eta\Delta_i^{\mathrm{new}};
$$

6. stop when

$$
\max_i |\Delta_i^{(k+1)}-\Delta_i^{(k)}|<\varepsilon.
$$

The mixing parameter $\eta$ is numerically important. A direct fixed-point update can oscillate or become unstable, especially near a gap-closing transition. Linear mixing damps the update and makes convergence more robust.

Physically, self-consistency captures the suppression of superconductivity by Zeeman polarization. A singlet superconductor pairs opposite spins. A Zeeman field tends to polarize spins, so the same field that drives the system toward a topological regime can also reduce the pairing amplitude. This is a key lesson of the self-consistent part of the project: the spectrum does not evolve over a passive, fixed superconducting background; the superconducting order itself responds to the parameters.

## 7. From self-consistent pairing to experimental proximitization

The final model changes the meaning of $\Delta(x)$. In many semiconductor-superconductor Majorana devices, the semiconductor is not intrinsically superconducting. It acquires pairing correlations by being coupled to a parent superconductor such as Al. In that situation, it is common to use an effective induced gap $\Delta_{\mathrm{ind}}(x)$ inside the semiconductor rather than computing the parent superconductor and the semiconductor self-consistently.

The numerical pipeline becomes

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{ind}}(x),\; V(x),\; \mu,\; E_Z,\; \alpha_R
\longrightarrow
H_{\mathrm{BdG}}
\longrightarrow
H_{\mathrm{BdG}}\psi_n=E_n\psi_n.
$$

The code includes a smooth superconducting coverage profile:

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{ind}}(x_i)=\Delta_0\frac{1}{2}
\left[1+\tanh\left(\frac{i-i_{\mathrm{SC}}}{s_{\mathrm{SC}}}\right)\right]e^{i\phi}.
$$

Here $i_{\mathrm{SC}}$ marks the beginning of the superconducting region and $s_{\mathrm{SC}}$ controls the smoothness of the interface. This is a phenomenological but useful representation of a device where the wire may have a small uncovered normal segment near one end.

The code also includes smooth electrostatic profiles. A Gaussian barrier is represented as

$$
V_i=V_0\exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{i-i_0}{\sigma}\right)^2\right].
$$

Such a barrier mimics the effect of a local gate near the end of the wire. This is not a cosmetic addition. Smooth confinement can generate Andreev bound states whose energy approaches zero and whose local signatures can resemble Majorana physics. These states are often called quasi-Majorana or partially separated Andreev bound states. They are a major reason why the project does not rely only on the lowest eigenenergy.

## 8. Numerical observables

The project computes several complementary observables.

### Minimum absolute energy

$$
E_{\min}=\min_n |E_n|.
$$

This is useful for detecting low-energy states, but it is not sufficient by itself. A trivial Andreev state can also produce small $E_{\min}$.

### Local density of states

The LDOS is approximated by Lorentzian broadening of discrete eigenstates:

$$
\rho(i,E)=\sum_n |\psi_n(i)|^2
\frac{\eta/\pi}{(E-E_n)^2+\eta^2}.
$$

It shows where low-energy spectral weight is located along the wire.

### Particle-hole density

For a BdG eigenstate,

$$
\rho(i)=\sum_{\sigma}\left(|u_{\sigma}(i)|^2+|v_{\sigma}(i)|^2\right).
$$

The separate electron and hole contributions help test whether a near-zero state has the balanced particle-hole character expected of a Majorana-like excitation.

### Edge weight

The code integrates the state density near the left and right ends. A strong edge weight is expected for Majorana modes, but one must also check whether the two Majorana components are separated rather than both localized near the same smooth potential well.

### BdG charge

A Majorana zero mode should be approximately charge neutral in the BdG sense. A diagnostic charge can be estimated from electron minus hole weight,

$$
Q_{\mathrm{BdG}}=\sum_i\left(|u(i)|^2-|v(i)|^2\right).
$$

For an ideal Majorana-like zero mode this quantity is close to zero, while ordinary finite-energy quasiparticles can carry stronger electron-like or hole-like character.

### Spin polarization

Spin diagnostics are useful in nanowires because the Zeeman field and Rashba coupling strongly shape the low-energy subspace. A low-energy state that evolves smoothly with Zeeman field can show characteristic spin polarization patterns, but spin alone is not a topological invariant.

## 9. Why the project is structured in three models

The three-stage structure is deliberate.

The Kitaev chain isolates the topology. It gives a clean analytical phase boundary, clear bulk-boundary correspondence, and transparent Majorana end modes.

The self-consistent BdG model introduces feedback. It shows that superconductivity is an order parameter determined by anomalous correlations, not just a fixed number placed in the Hamiltonian. This is useful for understanding what is lost when a fixed induced gap is assumed.

The proximitized nanowire model introduces device realism. It includes material parameters, Rashba spin-orbit coupling, Zeeman energy, induced superconductivity, coverage profiles, and electrostatic barriers. It also shows why finite-device Majorana identification is subtle: near-zero modes may appear for topological reasons, but they may also arise from smooth confinement and inhomogeneity.

Together, the models form a coherent path from ideal topology to practical numerical diagnostics.

## 10. Limitations

The simulations are intentionally transparent rather than optimized. The Hamiltonians are built as dense matrices and diagonalized with dense linear algebra. This is excellent for learning, validation, and figure generation at moderate system sizes, but it does not scale to very long wires. A production-grade solver would use sparse matrices and target only the low-energy eigenvalues.

The proximitized model uses an effective induced gap and simple electrostatic profiles. It does not solve a full three-dimensional Schrödinger-Poisson problem, does not include orbital magnetic effects, and does not explicitly model the parent superconductor. These are reasonable simplifications for a one-dimensional educational/research simulation, but they should be acknowledged when comparing with experiment.

The most important interpretive limitation is that a near-zero mode in a finite wire is not automatically a topological Majorana zero mode. The project therefore emphasizes multi-observable diagnosis rather than a single threshold or a single plot.
